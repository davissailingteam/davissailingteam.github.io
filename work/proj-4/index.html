Wind Forecasting

Sailing is a wind-dependent activity. While we'd love to go where the wind blows, we typically go to our port. So check out the wind forecast before you head to the port!

For a primer on wind, read the Origin of Wind.

The National Weather Service (NWS) provides a variety of forecast tools for us to use. Start with the Big Picture. Wind is driven by pressure gradients. Look at the isobars (lines of constant pressure). Is the pressure constant across the West? Sad times, no wind. Are the isobars really close together? Yay, wind!

Next, check out the NWS SAC Forecast Discussion for a plain-english overview of what's going on over the next few days.

A couple days out, move to the NWS Graphical Forecast to see general wind speeds and directions. And, finally, check out the NWS SAC Hourly Forecast the evening before or morning of. Note that the hourly prediction is typically only valid day-before and, even then, may be off.

All of the forecast products for Lake Washington are convieneintly summed up here: Lake Washington Forecast (NWS)

Sail Theory

Sails manipulate airflow to create lift and drag forces. Understanding the phenomena behind sail propulsion gives the sailor a better understanding of proper sail trim; of how to go fast. In the 1970s, Arvel Gentry, a Boeing Aerodynamicist, published a series of articles on sail theory in SAIL magazine. Start with "How Sails Really Work" and go from there.
